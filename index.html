Choice overload

Past a point, more options stop freeing people and start stalling them.

choice-overloaddecisioncurationdefault-nudge

one idea, 3 ways to see it →

Two grocery tasting tables stand side by side. Twenty-four jams pull a thick crowd that lingers and looks; six jams pull a thinner crowd, yet many more leave holding a jar.

At a wall of turnstiles the crowd bunches between lanes, weighs which to pick, and drifts back out. Where a single gate stands open, the same people walk straight through in a calm single file.

Under a rail crammed with hangers, someone stands stuck — nothing to wear. Beside it, a curated few, and an outfit chosen in a moment.

One idea, told three ways so it lands for any audience: past a point, more options stall people rather than free them — so curate a clear few and point to a default.

faced with too much, most people choose nothing.

The research. Iyengar & Lepper (2000) 'When Choice is Demotivating', J. Personality & Social Psychology — the jam-tasting field experiment (24 vs 6).

The subtraction blind spot

When we set out to improve something, we reflexively add — and overlook taking away, even when less is the better move.

subtractiondesigndecisionless-is-more

one idea, 3 ways to see it →

A see-saw tips, heavy on one side. Reaching to even it out, we stack new weights onto the light side and never think to lift one off the heavy one.

The old remote bristles with forty-odd buttons; the redesign keeps a quiet handful. The win came from deleting, not designing more.

A gardener cuts one branch from the plant. From the bare wound the plant pushes out fresh shoots and fruit, returning far more than was removed.

The same reflex, three ways: when something needs fixing, we pile on more — and miss the fix that removes.

adding comes first. Subtracting is the idea we have to reach for.

The research. Adams, Converse, Hales & Klotz (2021) 'People Systematically Overlook Subtractive Changes', Nature 592 — eight experiments — Lego grids, recipes, essays, schedules. Read the source →

Scarcity captures the mind

Having too little doesn't only cost you what you lack — it captures your attention and taxes everything else.

scarcityattentionbandwidthpovertyfocus

one idea, 3 ways to see it →

The same farmer's mind, poor before the harvest and flush after. Short on money, the IQ score falls about thirteen points, with nothing in the person changed.

The glass is already full of the scarce worry. The plan, the form, and the patience cannot fit, so they spill over the sides.

Scarcity throws a bright beam on the one urgent thing. Everything beside it sits in shadow.

Having too little narrows the mind to the lack — which helps in the moment, and quietly costs everything else.

when someone can't act, it's often not weak will. It's a mind already full.

The research. Mullainathan & Shafir (2013) 'Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much' — sugarcane farmers, poor before harvest vs. flush after (~13 IQ points). Read the source →

Why this exists

Complex ideas are hard to hold onto. A dense paper, a subtle finding, a tangled concept — most of it fades soon after you read it. Napkin Solo exists to change that: to take difficult ideas and make them easy to grasp and hard to forget. What reaches you is disarmingly simple — a single picture that makes an idea click and stays with you. Behind that simplicity is an AI-assisted engine we have spent a long time building and refining.

The origin of the name

Think of the best explanation anyone ever gave you — chances are it happened over a table, and someone reached for a napkin to draw it. When words won't do, we sketch. That napkin is the whole idea here: the shortest path from a tangled thought to oh, I get it.

And Solo — for Han Solo. The instinct to take the direct route, trust your own read of a thing, and pull it off with a little swagger. Complex ideas, drawn plainly, and without fuss.
